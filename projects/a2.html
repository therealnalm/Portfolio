Guiding Questions

Has the number of owner-occupied units decreased, or has the corporate ownership rate increased mostly from the purchase of newer units/housing for a growing population?

I’m curious to understand if families/individuals that have/would have historically owned (and occupied) their properties have been able to maintain ownership and/or pass property down through their family or if the market has taken such a dramatic direction that people who would have traditionally been owner-occupiers have given up ownership. From another lens, is the housing market’s inability to keep up with a growing population the main proponent of corporate ownership or is the rise in private equity and other real estate investment channels strong enough that it has pushed people out of ownership? I’m curious if there is an equity angle where only new homebuyers have been pushed out or if previous homeowners have also suffered.

How have different neighborhoods been affected by corporate ownership over time?

Although it’s clear (and unsurprising) that housing speculation has had the greatest effect in lower income neighborhoods, I’m curious if other neighborhoods have seen the same trends on a time-delayed basis or if the lower income neighborhoods have simply been affected more. Conversely, as investor ownership of property has grown in less-advantaged communities, has it saturated the market and flowed over into other areas or does there not seem to be an end in sight?

Who owns property across neighborhoods of different demographics?

Home ownership in the US has historically been disproportionately attained by white Americans. Given that Boston is a racially diverse city with specific neighborhoods often being more racially homogeneous, I’m curious to see if there is any relationship between property ownership and the demographics of different neighborhoods. This question was spurred from the MAPC reports statistics on how speculation has affected different demographics.

Exploration and Insights

To begin answering the first question, I calculated a new field to determine the number of owner occupied units corresponding with each year.

This graph shows the total number of owner occupied units (tot unit * Own Occ Rate) in Boston from 2004 to 2024. It shows an 11% decrease over 20 years which indicates that even accounting for population growth and new construction, the amount of owner occupied units is decreasing numerically before taking into account a growing population and housing stock. Next, I’d like to explore how this phenomenon manifested in different neighborhoods.

This graph displays the number of owner occupied units each year per neighborhood. At first glance, what stands out is the “flatness” of the lines and Dorchester being an outlier in magnitude. The flatness would show a more constant number of owner occupied units. Dorchester, for example, only experienced a 4% decrease over 20 years. That said, this effect is balanced out by other neighborhoods like Alston which experienced a 24% decrease. Also notable is South Boston Waterfront (Seaport) which experienced a 20% increase in owner occupied units, likely due to the massive developments built there during this time. At a glance, a 20% increase in owner occupied units within a region that probably doubled or tripled the total number of units seems surprisingly low and warrants more investigation. Upon further inspection, I also noticed that the number of owner occupied units in Dorchester flatlines between 2013 and 2020 at 23,023 units (.48 ooc rate * 47,965 units), which seems highly unrealistic. Since the owner occupied rate is rounded to 2 decimal places over 1000’s of units, it's possible that the rate did not change, but it is improbable the the total number of units would have also remained constant for 7 years. Similar abnormalities exist elsewhere, like West Roxbury from 2015 to 2021. To further explore this, I decided to look into the total number of units in Boston.

The following graph should show the total number of units available in Boston over the period in question. Although the bad news is it is also flatlined at 282,190 units, it means we found our problem in the data! Just to be sure, I checked the data at a neighborhood level.

This graph of total number of units in Boston at the neighborhood level confirms the issue. A cross check with data from the 2 most recent census shows that there were 272,481 units in 2010 and 301,702 units in 2020, indicating that the 282,190 units were likely either the housing from an intermediate year (2014?) or the average number of housing units over the 20 years.

Further direct inspection of the data (pictured left) shows that only the own_occ and corp_own fields are specific by year, with all other census fields held constant. Interestingly, these fields are selectively different from the data linked as the “source” in the file’s metadata (pictured right). Allston has different numbers while Beacon Hill is the same.

Nonetheless, this all shows that this dataset is not usable for any time-related statistics or graphs, except in relation to owner occupancy rate and corporate ownership rate. Fortunately the 2nd question only relies on that data, and the 3rd question can be roughly answered without year-specific data under the assumption that racial demographics of Boston’s neighborhoods have not undergone any dramatic changes over the past 20 years. That said, since the data to properly answer that question is available elsewhere, I’ll focus on answering the 2nd question.

To address the second question, I began by assessing the spatial quality of the data provided in tableau, using 2024 and 2004 as reference points.

The maps show the corporate ownership rate of properties throughout Boston in 2004 and 2024. That said, the dividing lines are confusing since many of the regions that are displayed as divided represent the same data. Additionally, 2 pieces of land around Back Bay and Northeastern are not included. Nonetheless, it shows some clear trends about corporate ownership. First: corp. ownership is higher in the parts of Boston that are further North. Specifically, Downtown and Seaport exhibit the highest rates of corporate ownership. This makes sense, given that they are both business districts with a lot of office space and high rises. It’s also worth noting that Allston and Downtown were the areas with the highest corporate ownership in 2004 and continued to have some of the highest rates in 2024. I was unable to discern what the unknown/null values were although I did exclude them for the following maps.

This map shows the delta between corporate ownership between 2004 to 2024 by neighborhood. Based on the assertions from the MAPC that more speculation occurs in low-income, non-white neighborhoods, which I understood are usually in the more southern parts of Boston, I was surprised that corporate ownership was higher in more northern areas. That said, it makes sense that Seaport would have the highest Delta given the rapid influx of large-scale, high-end development over the past 20 years. Overall, it seems possible that the highest deltas are in the neighborhoods that have experienced the most development over the past 20 years.

These maps show the owner occupied unit rate throughout Boston in 2004 and 2024. Somewhat unsurprisingly, they present as a slight inverse to the maps of corporate ownership rate in Boston. This makes sense, given that corporate owned units and owner occupied units are largely mutually exclusive. Interestingly, the two maps are relatively similar and do not seem to show much of a change in occupant ownership. Again, somewhat contradictory to what might have been implied by the MAPC report, it seems that Boston’s most diverse neighborhoods have higher home ownership rates than the less diverse neighborhoods.

This map shows the change in owner occupied unit rate in Boston between 2004 and 2024. This map paints a very different picture than would have been expected from the corporate ownership delta map. The changes are much smaller than would have been expected. Whereas I may have assumed a somewhat direct converse relationship between corporate ownership rate and owner occupant rate, their change does not appear to be correlated. The gap between high corporate ownership increases compared to smaller owner occupancy rate decreases, would likely point to one of two things. First, the two statistics do not refer to the same sum whole. The meta-data does not state that the statistics refer to the same source data. Second, and perhaps more likely, is that there are other ownership groups. Non-corporate, non-occupying owners make up a significant portion of property owners. Their ownership rate would fill the delta between the other 2 figures. This would indicate that these owners are likely the ones selling their properties to corporate owners.

Summary

I first discovered that this dataset is not reliable to answer questions related to annual changes in population or housing supply in Boston, but only accurate for data relating to owner occupation rates and corporate ownership rates of units in Boston. Second, there is a loose inverse relationship between rates of owner occupation and corporate ownership of units in Boston, although the 2 don’t necessarily maintain that relationship linearly over time. Lastly, surprisingly it seems that the wealthier parts of Boston seem to have higher rates of corporate ownership and lower rates of owner occupancy while less wealthy (and more diverse) parts of Boston seem to have lower corporate ownership rates and higher owner occupancy rates. This seems to run contrary to what was reported in the MAPC piece.
